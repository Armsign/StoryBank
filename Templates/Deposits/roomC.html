Each of the artefacts in this room are intimately connected to P.L. Travers life, experience, philosophy and ambition.

For many years, Mervyn the Rocking Horse resided in the entrance hall of her town-house, establishing an air of child-like whimsy the moment one walked through the front door.

No one really knows the exact reason why P.L. Travers chose the name Mervyn.

But we do know that she was close friends with Mervyn Peake, an author famous for his Gormenghast trilogy and illustrations he created for authors such as Lewis Carroll, Robert Louis Stevenson, Balzac and the Brothers Grimm.

Do any of your childhood possessions inspire story ideas?

In her stories, P.L. Travers often personified things - the literary technique of giving human qualities to inanimate objects. For example, she believed her house in Chelsea to be sad and disconsolate until she moved in and created her own world inside.

She famously painted the front door of 29 Shawfield Street the pretty colour of cherry blossoms, the same colour used for the cover of Mary Poppins in Cherry Tree Lane.

Describe the neighbourhood where you live. What stories, real or imagined, take place there?

Royal Doulton manufactured china plates, bowls and figurines featuring many characters and memorable scenes from popular stories.

Her Great Aunt Ellie once gave P.L. Travers a Royal Doulton bowl as a birthday gift.

In one of the Mary Poppins' stories, the Banks children enter a scene hand-painted on the inside of a Royal Doulton ceramic bowl.

P.L. Travers was deeply interested in Zen philosophy, Buddhism and Hindu teachings and other spiritual influences.

She believed that stories arose from the mysterious places of the soul, bubbling up into the conscious mind during the quiet moments of meditation and reflection.
